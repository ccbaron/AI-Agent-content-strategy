# Competitive Observations — Internal Notes

**Applies to:** Positioning decisions, messaging calibration, proposal writing, agent context  
**Last reviewed:** Q2 2026

---

## Why we keep these notes

Not to obsess over competitors. To stay calibrated. The risk in this niche is that you start drifting toward the same language everyone else uses because it sounds credible — and you don't notice until nothing you say sounds distinct anymore.

These are practical notes: phrases that show up too often, claims that feel hollow, gaps we keep seeing. It's easier to say something different if you have a clear picture of what everyone else is already saying.

---

## Recurring messaging patterns

Spend an afternoon on competitor websites and a few patterns appear within the first ten minutes. None of these are dishonest. They're just everywhere.

**"We sit at the intersection of [X] and [Y]."**  
Usually "strategy and technology" or "creativity and data" or some variation of "AI and human insight." The intersection framing sounds considered but it's become a generic structure. Two things overlapping doesn't explain what specifically happens at that overlap or why it produces better work.

**"We help ambitious companies [vague outcome]."**  
Ambition is apparently the minimum qualifying criterion for every studio's client roster. "Bold," "visionary," and "forward-thinking" appear with similar frequency. None of these describe a business problem or a type of client in any way a real prospect can use.

**"Human-centered + [technology/AI/data/strategy]."**  
This probably was meaningful at some point — when the concern about impersonal automation felt new. In 2026 it reads as defensive boilerplate. The modifier is there because someone worried about sounding cold and never removed it.

**"We don't just deliver [X] — we deliver [X] that drives results."**  
The implied claim that other firms deliver X without results is never backed up. And "drives results" has been hollowed out by enough repetition that nothing around it benefits from the phrase.

**"Tailored to your unique needs."**  
Used so universally it communicates the opposite. Nobody explains what tailoring actually involves or what it requires from the client. It's furniture, not information.

**"We're a team of strategists, creatives, and technologists."**  
A staffing description, not a position. It says who is involved, not what they produce, or why this specific combination produces better work than another studio's version of the same three categories.

## What most competitors say that feels interchangeable

The problem isn't usually dishonesty. It's convergence. Everyone has pressure-tested their language toward safety — nothing too specific, nothing that limits inbound — and the result is an undifferentiated mass of reasonable-sounding copy.

Strip the company name from most premium studio homepages and you can't identify the firm from the text alone. The language manages risk but sacrifices any reason for a specific reader to care.

Phrases that survive this convergence most reliably:

- "We partner with you to..." — sounds collaborative, functions as overhead
- "Our proven approach..." — proven where, shown how?
- "We bring clarity to complexity" — used often enough to now suggest complexity in its own right
- "End-to-end [service]" — implies breadth, usually means generalism
- "Transformative outcomes" — the cautious cousin of "game-changing," equally empty
- "Trusted by leading companies in [sector]" — name them or cut it

The deeper issue: most firm websites are written as if the reader has never heard of their category and needs everything explained gently. In practice, the clients worth having are already familiar with the space. They're not looking for orientation — they're looking for a reason to believe this firm in particular understands their specific problem.

## What kinds of claims sound credible

When a competitor says something that actually lands, it tends to have at least one of these properties:

**It describes a mechanism, not just a result.** "We shorten the time between insight and decision by embedding research into the strategy phase" says something about how the work is structured. "We deliver faster results" says nothing.

**It acknowledges a constraint.** Any firm willing to write "this works best when X — if you're in Y, it's probably not the right fit" reads as dramatically more credible than one implying universal applicability. The constraint is the signal.

**It uses the client's vocabulary for the problem.** Not "we improve brand equity" but "we work with companies spending on paid acquisition who aren't seeing it convert." The latter shows familiarity with how the problem actually feels from the inside.

**It defends a position that someone might push back on.** Firms with a visible intellectual stance are easier to trust. A claim that requires defending means someone thought about it. A claim everyone agrees with is usually not doing much.

**It's narrow enough to be plausible.** "We've worked with 200+ brands across 15 industries" is less credible than "we've spent most of the last three years working with B2B SaaS companies preparing for Series A or B." The narrower claim makes the expertise feel like it actually exists.

## What kinds of claims feel inflated

These show up constantly. A skeptical reader stops believing everything around them once one appears.

**"We've helped companies achieve 10x growth."** Any multiplier without context. 10x what, from what baseline, over what period, with what other contributing factors? This kind of claim brackets everything near it.

**Future-tense transformation language.** "We'll transform how you think about your brand" — a commitment no one can make before seeing the work. Strong firms describe what has happened, not what will. Prospects have heard this promise before and it rarely delivered.

**"Leading" as a self-applied adjective.** "We are a leading strategy firm." Leading by whose measure? This phrase got into the copy because it sounded credible and nobody removed it.

**Category definitions that include the firm's own method.** "True strategy is [description that maps exactly to how we work]." This circular move defines the category so the firm wins by definition. Readers recognize it as marketing, not insight.

**Volume without specificity.** "Over 500 clients" — doing what for whom? Volume without context usually signals breadth without depth, or work the firm can't describe in specific terms.

**"Proprietary methodology."** Unless the methodology is actually explained and is genuinely distinct, this means "we gave our standard consulting process a brand name." Being willing to explain how you actually work, in plain language, is more impressive.

## Where competitors overuse AI language

The most active category right now and the one most likely to date badly.

**"AI-powered" as a standalone claim.** By 2026 this is roughly equivalent to "internet-enabled" in 2005 — a baseline, not a differentiator. Firms leading with "AI-powered strategy" or "AI-native consulting" are making a category claim, not a differentiation claim. The only useful version of this conversation is: what does the AI specifically enable that clients couldn't access before?

**Stacking AI vocabulary without grounding.** "We leverage large language models, generative AI, and machine learning to deliver intelligent insights across the client journey." This sentence was reviewed by someone who thought thoroughness would land. It reads as a features list that doesn't connect to any specific problem a client has.

**"AI as a team member."** Some firms position AI as a collaborative partner or always-on analyst. It sounds modern but mostly raises concerns rather than resolving them. Clients want to know who's accountable for the judgment — and "the AI and I are a team" doesn't answer that.

**Speed as the primary AI benefit.** "AI lets us deliver faster." True but underargued. Speed is a secondary benefit. The better case is research quality, depth of pattern recognition, and the ability to hold more context simultaneously. Firms that only talk about speed are leaving the stronger argument on the table.

**Basic automation described as AI strategy.** "We use AI to generate first drafts, summarize research, and produce content at scale." This is commodity tooling. Saying you use AI to summarize research is like saying you use search engines to find information. True, but not a reason to hire this firm.

The firms that use AI language well describe specifically what AI does in their process, are honest about what it doesn't do, and are clear that the judgment layer is still human. That combination — precision, honesty, demarcation — is rare enough that when it appears it reads as credible.

## Gaps and opportunities

Things that would be genuinely useful to an evaluating client that almost no firm provides.

**Honest scope limits.** Almost no firm says publicly who shouldn't hire them. The firms that do — briefly, specifically, without apology — read as significantly more credible. If you can afford to turn work away, you must be doing something right.

**Addressing the decision-maker's actual concern.** Most websites talk about outputs and outcomes. Almost none address what a decision-maker is actually worried about before signing: whether the work will produce something usable; whether the engagement will drain more time than it saves; whether the firm will push back or just agree. Naming these directly is unusual enough to be distinctive.

**Post-engagement framing.** "After working with us, you'll have X" is more useful than "we deliver X." The former is from the client's experience. Almost nobody does this clearly.

**An honest, plain-language description of how AI is used.** Most firms are either overselling it or burying it because they're not sure how clients will react. The credible version describes it matter-of-factly: here's what we use it for, here's what we still do ourselves, here's why the combination is better than either alone.

**Content that requires defending.** The current competitive content landscape is effectively a mutual non-aggression pact. Everyone publishes something that sounds smart and nobody says anything another firm would push back on. A studio willing to take a real position on something contested — something that implies other approaches are wrong — stands out because of the seriousness, not the contrarianism.

**Naming what an engagement doesn't produce.** Specifying the limits of the work up front, in a proposal or on the site, immediately filters for the right client and reads as honest. Almost no firm does this.

## Behavioral patterns worth watching

**The authority laundering cycle.** A firm publishes a piece, references it in a later piece as evidence of their thinking, cites their own thinking as a credential in a proposal. Not dishonest, but the chain of authority traces entirely back to themselves. The firms worth watching are the ones whose thinking gets referenced by people outside the firm.

**The rebrand without repositioning.** New visual identity, cleaner copy, same fundamental problem: trying to serve too broad an audience with too undifferentiated an offer. The new site looks sharper. The underlying tension doesn't go away.

**Polished case studies with minimal content.** Beautiful photography, vague outcomes, no real information. Production value as a substitute for specificity. This pattern is common enough that it's started to read as a signal something is being hidden — either the client didn't want to be named, or the outcome wasn't worth describing.

---

## Implications for how we present

**Name the constraint.** Almost no competitor says explicitly who shouldn't hire them. We should — briefly and without apology.

**Describe what the client has after the engagement, from their perspective.** Not deliverable names. What do they now know or have that they didn't before, and what can they do with it?

**Describe AI's role precisely without making it the headline.** The credibility is in how we describe it, not how prominently we feature it.

**Publish something that requires defending.** Not to be provocative — because it's the only honest signal that there's a real point of view here.

**Let substance lead.** Everything at this level should look clean and professional. The distinction comes from the quality of thinking, not from production polish that any agency can achieve.
