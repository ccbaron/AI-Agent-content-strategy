# Brand Voice Guide — Internal Reference

**Applies to:** All written outputs — website copy, proposals, social content, reports, presentations, agent responses  
**Last reviewed:** Q2 2026

---

## What this document is for

A working reference, not a rulebook. The goal isn't uniformity of phrasing — it's consistency of judgment: what we choose to say, what we don't say, and how we calibrate tone depending on who we're talking to.

If something reads well but doesn't match this guide, use your judgment. If it matches the guide but feels off, it's probably off.

---

## How the brand should sound

**Sharp.** Sentences that say something specific. No padding, no throat-clearing, no "in today's fast-paced environment." If a sentence doesn't make a point, cut it. The fastest way to tell if a sentence is doing work: remove it and see if the paragraph still reads. If it does, delete the sentence.

**Credible.** Trust comes through precision, not credentials. Reference concrete things — decisions, trade-offs, observed patterns — not vague assertions of expertise. "We help companies grow" says nothing. "We help early-stage companies sharpen messaging before they start spending on acquisition" says something.

**Calm.** Not cold — calm. No urgency theater. No "act now," no manufactured friction. When urgency exists it's factual: "the pitch is in three weeks" or "the competitor launched last month." We don't manufacture it.

**Commercially aware.** Every claim connects back to a decision, a cost, a constraint. Strategy without commercial grounding is just opinion. This means naming what something costs — budget, attention, time, other priorities — not only what it might deliver. "This will improve brand clarity" isn't a commercial observation. "Spending on paid before positioning is clear tends to increase CAC in the short term" is.

**Modern without performing it.** No gratuitous AI references. No "leverage the power of." Modern here means structurally clean, intellectually current, written without clichés. Not a collection of words that were trending six months ago.

---

## How the brand should not sound

**Not consultancy-speak.** "Synergies," "best practices," "holistic approach," "end-to-end solutions" — these signal either a lack of specificity or an attempt to obscure the absence of it. Some clients use this language internally, which is fine. We don't mirror it back at them.

**Not startup-hype.** The brand is not a pitch deck. It doesn't "revolutionize," "disrupt," or "unlock potential." If the offer is actually strong, plain language is more convincing than inflation. Founders especially see through it fast — they've been pitched too many times.

**Not academic.** We don't explain things at length to prove knowledge. That said, there's a version of this worth watching: the casual "as you know..." framing that skips necessary context can read as arrogant rather than efficient. The goal is efficient, not dismissive.

**Not too casual.** Conversational syntax is fine. But slang, exclamation marks, and loosely structured "here are some thoughts" writing don't hold up at the premium end. More essay, less Slack message.

**Not self-referential.** "We are a leading strategic studio" is weaker than a sentence that describes what changed for a specific client. The work speaks; the descriptor doesn't.

## Preferred wording patterns

Tendencies, not templates.

- Active verbs with clear subjects. "We map the competitive landscape" is cleaner than "Competitive landscape analysis is provided." The passive construction almost always signals a writer avoiding commitment.
- Specific categories over broad ones. "B2B SaaS founders pre-Series A" beats "innovative companies." "Operators running teams of 10–30" beats "business leaders."
- Name the tension. "Most companies default to broad messaging because specificity feels risky. It usually isn't." This is more useful than advice that has no friction to it.
- Outcomes framed as decisions. "After this you'll know what to prioritize and what to stop" beats "we deliver actionable insights." The client cares what they'll do differently, not what we deliver.
- Hedged confidence where it's earned. "In most cases," "the pattern we see most often," "this depends on whether X or Y" — honesty about scope is a trust signal, not a weakness. Overconfident claims in proposals are one of the fastest ways to lose a room.
- Short sentences after weight. Long sentences carry nuance. Short ones land it.

## Phrases to avoid

These phrases appear frequently in brand copy and are either overused, empty, or actively misleading. None of them should appear without a specific reason.

| Phrase | Problem |
|---|---|
| "Unlock your potential" | Vague. Could mean anything. |
| "Best-in-class" | Unsubstantiated superlative. |
| "Leverage" (as a verb, in most uses) | Jargon. Use "use," "apply," or "build on." |
| "Holistic approach" | Signals vagueness, not comprehensiveness. |
| "Game-changer" | Overused. Never believed. |
| "Cutting-edge" | Meaningless. What edge? |
| "We're passionate about..." | Irrelevant. Clients care about what you can do. |
| "In today's landscape..." | Empty opener. Cut it and start with the point. |
| "Empower teams to..." | Vague agency. What does the team actually do differently? |
| "AI-powered" (as a standalone claim) | Table stakes in 2026. Say what the AI does specifically. |

## Weak vs. strong messaging — examples

### Example 1: Introducing a service

**Weak:**  
"We offer a comprehensive content strategy service designed to help brands achieve their communication goals in a crowded digital landscape."

**Strong:**  
"We help companies figure out what they're actually saying — and whether it's landing with the people who should buy from them."

---

### Example 2: Describing an outcome

**Weak:**  
"Our process delivers actionable insights that drive measurable results."

**Strong:**  
"At the end of the engagement, you'll have a positioning framework, a clear content hierarchy, and a short list of things to stop doing."

---

### Example 3: Talking about AI

**Weak:**  
"We leverage cutting-edge AI technology to supercharge your content strategy."

**Strong:**  
"We use AI to compress the research phase and surface patterns faster — the strategic judgment is still ours."

---

### Example 4: Headline for a launch post

**Weak:**  
"Introducing Content Intelligence Agent — the future of AI-powered strategy."

**Strong:**  
"We built a research and strategy agent for the part of the work that takes the most time and produces the least output."

---

### Example 5: A positioning claim

**Weak:**  
"We're different because we combine creativity with data."

**Strong:**  
"Most strategy work either ignores the numbers or drowns in them. We try to hold both."

## Speaking to different audiences

The voice doesn't change. The entry point does. Register stays consistent — calm, precise, commercially oriented. What shifts is where we start.

### Founders

They evaluate quickly and have a low tolerance for overhead. Lead with the problem or the decision, not the method. They don't need our process explained upfront — they need to decide in the first two minutes if this conversation is worth their time.

Avoid long preambles. Avoid academic framing. Don't hand-hold.

Lead with: the specific friction, the cost of not resolving it, what a decision actually requires.

Example that works: *"If you're spending on paid acquisition and conversion is flat, you probably have a positioning problem, not a traffic problem. The copy is just making the positioning more visible."*

---

### Operators — heads of department, senior team leads, senior ICs

They've sat through enough strategy engagements that produced no output. They're skeptical of frameworks that don't survive contact with their team's actual constraints. What earns trust here is specificity about what the work involves, what's required from their side, and what can go wrong.

Avoid: abstract benefits, outcomes that sound easy, vague timelines.

Lead with: what the engagement realistically looks like, what it needs from them, what the honest output is.

Example that works: *"This works well if there's a clear owner on your side and some flexibility in the brief. If the messaging is already locked by someone above you, we'd scope differently — probably start with an audit and present findings before touching the positioning."*

---

### Marketing leads

They live between strategy and execution. They often already know what the problem is — they need either ammunition to secure internal buy-in, or a faster path to output than they can get internally right now. They respond well to specific frameworks, examples from adjacent situations, and language that connects to things they already track.

Avoid: explaining things they already know. Avoid framing our work as "consulting" when what they want is a decision and a clear output.

Lead with: the specific lever, what changes in practice, how they'll know if it's working.

Example that works: *"The issue usually isn't volume — most teams are publishing enough. It's that the content hierarchy isn't clear, so nothing pulls harder than anything else. You end up with a library that feels busy but doesn't move anyone."*

## Closing

A few things worth stating plainly:

Every draft should pass a specificity check. If a sentence could appear in anyone's copy without changing a word, it probably shouldn't appear in ours.

Credibility is demonstrated through what you say, not claimed through how you describe yourself. The copy should show judgment, not assert it.

Tension is a trust signal. Naming what's hard, what has trade-offs, or what doesn't always work makes the brand more credible, not less. Clients are not naive and they've read enough polished copy to recognize when something is being softened.

If it sounds like it came from a tool — not an imperfect artifact, but the particular kind of smooth, hedged, complete-sounding prose that tools produce — rewrite it. The standard isn't grammatically correct. It's "would a thoughtful person find this worth reading."
