# Ideal Customer Profile — Internal Memo

**Applies to:** Business development, proposal writing, positioning, agent context  
**Last reviewed:** Q2 2026

---

## What this document is for

A working definition of who we're building for. Not personas — those flatten into demographics and stop being useful. The goal here is to understand what actually drives someone to hire a studio like this, what they're afraid of, and what kind of engagement they'll find valuable versus frustrating.

Use this when writing proposals, evaluating inbound, or calibrating how to frame an offer.

---

## Primary customer segments

### 1. Founders scaling from traction to growth

Post-PMF, somewhere between seed and Series B — they have a product that works and early customers who are genuinely happy. The problem is the messaging hasn't kept up. The founder wrote the first homepage copy two years ago when the product was half-built, and it still reads like a pitch deck. Paid acquisition is getting expensive and conversion is flat or inconsistent. They suspect positioning is the problem but don't have bandwidth or the right outside view to fix it cleanly.

What they want is not a rebrand. They want to get sharper without losing what's already working — and they're often worried that an outside firm will strip out the one thing that is actually resonating. That fear is worth acknowledging early.

**Company size:** 5–40 people  
**Stage:** Post-product-market fit, pre-scaling  
**Decision-maker:** Founder or co-founder, occasionally Head of Marketing if the role exists and that person has real authority

---

### 2. Small operators building a presence in a specific niche

Consultants, boutique service firms, specialists — often solo or a small team. Good at what they do, mostly invisible in the market. They're not on a venture track. Growth means higher rates, better clients, fewer conversations about price.

The positioning problem usually takes one of two forms: they've never invested in it and are essentially starting from scratch, or they've accumulated messaging over years that no longer coheres — the website says one thing, the proposal says another, and they default to explaining themselves from scratch in every sales call. Either way they need to make an actual choice about what they stand for before communication will improve.

These clients tend to be thoughtful and opinionated, which makes the work easier and sometimes harder. They have a point of view on their own work — they just struggle to translate it into something a new client can evaluate quickly.

**Company size:** 1–10 people  
**Sector:** B2B services, professional services, creative industries, emerging tech  
**Decision-maker:** Owner or managing partner

---

### 3. Marketing or strategy leads inside small-to-mid companies

Not the final decision-maker but close. They're the person who already has a working diagnosis of what needs to change — they just need external capacity, a specific capability they don't have in-house, or something with enough authority to move an internal conversation forward.

They bring us in for contained engagements: a repositioning sprint, a competitive analysis, a content strategy that actually maps to the business model, AI-assisted research at a scale their team can't run. The scope is usually well-defined, which is often more pleasant to work on than sprawling founder-led projects — but the internal politics can be messier.

**Company size:** 20–150 people  
**Role:** Head of Marketing, Strategy Lead, Content Director, Brand Manager  
**Decision-maker:** Has budget or real influence over it — important to know which before scoping

## Typical frustrations

These are the recurring complaints that surface before someone looks for help. They rarely say all of them directly, but they're almost always somewhere in the conversation.

- **"We've worked with agencies before and it went nowhere."** The deliverable looked polished but produced no actual output. They got a deck, a workshop, a framework — none of which survived contact with the team's real work.
- **"We don't have bandwidth to do this internally."** Strategy work keeps getting pushed because nothing urgent is breaking yet. Until something does.
- **"Our messaging is inconsistent and nobody knows why."** The website says one thing, the sales deck says another, the founder says a third. Nobody in the room can explain how it got this way.
- **"We're too close to it."** They've been inside the product so long they've lost track of what the first-time reader actually sees.
- **"We've tried AI tools and gotten mediocre results."** They've run the obvious experiments, the outputs were generic, and now they're skeptical — but not so skeptical they've stopped looking.
- **"Our competitors communicate better even though their product is worse."** The most common trigger. It also tends to make the internal conversation about messaging more politically charged than it should be.

## Buying triggers

These are the events or realizations that turn latent awareness into an actual conversation.

- Conversion rates drop or plateau despite traffic growth
- A competitor launches with cleaner messaging and starts winning deals
- A fundraise or major pitch is coming up and the current narrative doesn't hold
- A new product line or expansion requires a positioning decision
- The team reaches a size where informal communication breaks down and consistency becomes a real problem
- They read, see, or hear something that makes them realize their positioning is exactly the wrong thing — a client says something revealing, a lost deal surfaces the real objection
- AI is now visibly central to their industry and they haven't figured out how to integrate it credibly

## Objections before hiring

These are worth taking seriously, not deflecting. The best clients raise them. If the answer isn't honest, the relationship starts wrong.

**"We've done this before and it didn't change anything."**  
Usually means the deliverable wasn't operationalized. It lived in a folder, was referenced once in a meeting, and was never touched again. Legitimate concern. The answer is not "we're different" — it's explaining specifically what the engagement produces and in what form. If we can't describe that clearly, we haven't scoped the work properly yet.

**"We're not ready — we need to resolve a few things first."**  
Sometimes true. Often a delay that will repeat indefinitely. The right move is to ask what specifically needs to be true before positioning work makes sense. More often than not, the answer reveals they're ready — they're just nervous about committing to a position and excluding something.

**"We don't have budget right now."**  
Occasionally accurate. More often it means the commercial case wasn't made clearly enough in the proposal. If budget is the only thing named as a blocker and the engagement is otherwise compelling, something in the framing of value didn't land.

**"Can't we handle this internally?"**  
They can. They haven't. The intention has been there for months and keeps getting deprioritized by things that feel more urgent. That's not a knock on the team — it's how internal strategy work goes. The value of external engagement includes the accountability that forces the conversation to actually happen.

**"We need someone who knows our industry."**  
This is worth exploring rather than dismissing. Sometimes it's a real requirement — particularly in highly regulated sectors or in categories where the positioning landscape is unusually specific. More often, positioning problems are structural, and a good diagnostic question will reveal more than vertical familiarity will. Worth asking: what would industry knowledge actually change about the engagement?

## What they secretly want but don't say directly

This is the most useful section for proposal writing and for understanding what "success" actually means to a client.

- They want to feel like someone finally understands their business clearly enough to articulate it better than they can.
- They want to be challenged — but by someone they respect. Not challenged arbitrarily.
- They want the engagement to produce conviction, not just options. They're tired of "it depends" that doesn't resolve into a recommendation.
- They want something they can use immediately — a framework, a statement, a hierarchy — not a document they need to interpret.
- They want to stop second-guessing their messaging every time they write something.
- They want to look credible externally without having to overclaim.
- For AI-related work: they want to understand what's actually possible without being sold a transformation narrative they don't believe.

## Language that resonates

These are words and framings that land well with this audience. Not because they're fashionable but because they match the actual problem.

**Resonates:**
- "Get clear on" — implies active resolution, not passive exposure
- "The actual problem" / "what's really going on" — signals they're not going to get a surface-level answer
- "Useful," "practical," "operational" — they've been burned by beautiful but useless deliverables
- "You probably already know the answer, but..." — treats them as intelligent, not as an audience for a pitch
- "Trade-off" — they respect honesty about costs and constraints
- "Short list" — specific, manageable output implied
- "What to kill" — signals the work will include subtractive thinking, not just additions
- "Before you scale" — implies timing awareness, not generic advice

**Falls flat:**
- "Holistic strategy" — too vague, sounds like everything and nothing
- "Innovative solutions" — they've been burned by this language too many times
- "We'll work closely with your team to..." — sounds like overhead, not output
- "Roadmap" used loosely — they've had too many roadmaps that went nowhere
- "Empower you to..." — they want the thing done or clearly designed, not to be empowered

## Good fit vs. bad fit signals

### Signs of a good fit

- They can describe their problem with some specificity — not just "we need better marketing"
- They've thought about it before and have a point of view, even if it's incomplete
- They're making a real decision in the next 90 days that depends on having this figured out
- They push back on assumptions during the first conversation — that's curiosity, not obstruction
- They talk about their customers with nuance, not just enthusiasm
- When asked "what would make this successful?", they give a concrete answer
- They understand that positioning work is a choice — and that making a choice means ruling things out

### Signs of a bad fit

- They want the studio to tell them what business to be in — that's a different kind of work
- They have a strong opinion about the output before seeing any analysis ("we just need better copy")
- The decision-maker is not in the room (or the conversation) — someone is acting as a proxy with no authority
- They've done this three times in the past year with no implementation — there's a structural problem inside the organization, not a vendor problem
- The brief is purely executional with no room for strategic input
- They measure success by volume of deliverables, not quality of decisions
- They want to avoid committing to a specific position — they want messaging that "works for everyone"

## Practical implications

**The first conversation is diagnostic.** The goal is to understand the real trigger — not to pitch hours. A founder who opens with "we need a content strategy" is often actually worried about a competitor or a deal they lost. Get to the actual thing.

**Fit requires the decision-maker in the room.** If we're scoping something meaningful and the person we're talking to has no authority to say yes, we're not in the right conversation yet. Better to name this early than scope something that gets blocked internally.

**The best clients expect to be challenged.** They're paying for judgment, not agreement. Softening every observation to avoid friction produces weak work and clients who don't trust the output. The challenge is to do it without being combative — direct, not aggressive.

**Clients who count deliverables are a flag.** The clients worth having ask "what changes after this?" or "how will we know it worked?" The ones who ask for page counts and template numbers are usually managing the engagement from a procurement frame, which is a mismatch with how this work functions.

**"Not ready" is often just fear of committing.** If someone believes they need to finish the product before working on messaging — push on that. Usually the product is far enough along. What they're actually avoiding is the moment of choosing a position and ruling things out.
